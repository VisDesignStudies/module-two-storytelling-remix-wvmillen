Reading the Weather at Gale Crater

What Curiosity’s weather station can tell us about daily swings, Martian seasons, and the limits of measuring climate from one rover.

Working thesis

Weather, not a full climate map

The patterns are real and useful, but they are still filtered through one rover, one crater, and a dataset with visible gaps.

Orientation

Before the charts: two Mars time terms worth knowing.

A sol is a Martian day. NASA pegs it at about 24 hours, 39 minutes, and 35 seconds, so it runs a little longer than a day on Earth. Curiosity’s weather station, REMS, records each sol as one local slice of Gale Crater weather.

Ls is short for areocentric solar longitude. In plain language, it tracks where Mars sits in its orbit, which makes it a cleaner season marker than a simple month number. I use Ls below because it helps connect what happens over short windows of sols to the larger rhythm of a Martian year.

Sol

A Martian day. Slightly longer than an Earth day, so the rover’s clock slowly drifts against ours.

Ls

A 0° to 360° season marker around Mars’s orbit, with quarter turns at 0, 90, 180, and 270 degrees.

REMS

The Rover Environmental Monitoring Station on Curiosity, which measured local temperature, pressure, and other weather-related conditions in Gale Crater.

1. Daily swings

Average temperature is the wrong first question.

If I collapsed Gale Crater into a single average, I’d miss the part that feels most Mars-like: the daily spread. In this file, the typical gap between a sol’s low and high is about 64°C, and the widest swing reaches 85°C.

2. Seasonal structure

Pressure makes the Martian year easier to see.

The first chart moves through time in sols. This circular chart remaps the same weather record into orbital position. Angle is Ls, and radius is average pressure within each seasonal bin. The light line shows the full dataset; the darker line echoes whatever sol window is currently selected above.

That comparison matters because a local stretch of sols can look noisy on its own. Once it is placed on the seasonal chart, the pressure cycle becomes easier to read as part of a broader annual pattern.

Interaction: make a selection in chart 1, then hover a seasonal segment here to compare its full-dataset average with the selected window.

Pressure Through the Martian Year

Curiosity sits south of Mars’s equator, so the familiar season labels map a little differently than a northern-hemisphere Earth calendar. The point here is the cycle, not a perfect Earth analogy.

3. What got recorded

The measurement record is part of the weather story too.

The first two charts show patterns in the published values. This one backs up and asks a more basic question: what was actually recorded, field by field, across the mission timeline?

That matters because the dataset is not a clean, uninterrupted log. Temperature and pressure are mostly present, but there are visible gaps, and the public wind_speed field is effectively missing here. Instead of treating that as a footnote, this chart makes the recording pattern visible.

Interaction: hover anywhere on the chart to inspect one sol, and use the selected range from chart 1 to compare a focused window with the full mission record.

What Curiosity Actually Recorded

Rows are fields in the public file and columns are sols. Darker marks mean a value was reported for that sol.

This makes the data limits concrete. The local weather patterns are still worth exploring, but the mission record is uneven and the public wind field is not usable as a continuous time series here.

4. Data notes and limits

What these charts can support, and what they cannot.

The strongest part of this dataset is its long run of local observations at one place. The weakest part is also that same specificity. These views are useful for exploring Gale Crater, not for claiming that all Martian weather works the same way everywhere.

One rover, one location

Every row comes from Curiosity at Gale Crater, just south of the equator. That is a meaningful local record, not a planet-wide climate survey.

Published as outreach data

The Pudding repo quotes the original Centro de Astrobiología disclaimer directly: this file is intended for outreach, and other uses should be approached with caution.

Operational and timing effects

The documentation says REMS does not measure continuously, can sample at different times from one sol to the next, and can be influenced by rover position, shade, orientation, and dust on the rover.

Missingness is part of the story

This CSV has 27 rows with missing temperature or pressure values, and the published wind_speed field is effectively unusable here. The metadata notes wind transmission limits and marks missing values as NaN.
